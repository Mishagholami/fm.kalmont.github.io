Cold Weather Wilderness Safety

HYPOTHERMIA

Hypothermia occurs when your body loses heat faster than it can produce it, causing a dangerously low body temperature. It's a serious condition that requires immediate attention.

Symptoms:

  • Shivering (early stage)
  • Confusion and drowsiness
  • Slurred speech
  • Weak pulse and shallow breathing
  • Loss of coordination

Prevention:

  • Dress in layers with moisture-wicking base layers
  • Stay dry - change wet clothing immediately
  • Carry emergency shelter and extra insulation
  • Stay hydrated and maintain energy with regular snacks
  • Be aware of weather conditions and plan accordingly

How to Recognize the Signs of Avalanche Risk

  • Recent Snowfall: Most avalanches occur during or shortly after heavy snowfall
  • Temperature Changes: Rapid warming can destabilize snow layers
  • Visible Signs: Cracks forming in the snow as you walk, hollow sounds underfoot
  • Terrain Features: Slopes between 30-45 degrees are most prone to avalanches
  • Recent Avalanche Activity: Evidence of recent slides indicates unstable conditions

Signs & Stages of Hypothermia

Mild Hypothermia (32-35°C / 90-95°F)

  • Shivering
  • Cold, pale skin
  • Increased heart rate and breathing
  • Difficulty performing complex tasks

Moderate Hypothermia (28-32°C / 82-90°F)

  • Violent shivering or shivering stops
  • Confusion and irrational behavior
  • Slurred speech
  • Poor coordination and stumbling
  • Drowsiness and apathy

Severe Hypothermia (below 28°C / 82°F)

  • No shivering
  • Unconsciousness
  • Very slow, weak, or absent pulse
  • Rigid muscles
  • May appear dead

Who is at Risk of Hypothermia?

Anyone exposed to cold environments without adequate protection is at risk, but certain factors increase vulnerability:

Elderly people and young children have less efficient temperature regulation systems

People with certain medical conditions like hypothyroidism, diabetes, and malnutrition

Those taking certain medications that affect body temperature regulation

Avalanche Safety

Understanding avalanche terrain and conditions is essential for winter mountain travel. Always check local avalanche forecasts before heading out.

Essential Avalanche Safety Equipment:

  • Avalanche transceiver (beacon)
  • Probe
  • Shovel
  • Avalung or airbag pack (recommended)
  • Communication device

Avalanche Rescue Protocol:

  1. Ensure your own safety first
  2. Mark the last seen point of the victim
  3. Switch beacons to search mode
  4. Search systematically using beacon signals
  5. Use probe to pinpoint victim's location
  6. Dig strategically to reach victim quickly

Using Ice Axes

An ice axe is a versatile tool for mountaineering and winter hiking. Learning proper techniques is essential for safety.

Basic Ice Axe Techniques:

  • Self-arrest: Stopping a slide on snow or ice
  • Self-belay: Preventing a fall before it happens
  • Ascending: Using the axe for balance and as a third point of contact
  • Descending: Controlling your descent on steep terrain
  • Anchoring: Creating temporary anchors in snow

Practice these techniques in a safe environment before relying on them in critical situations.

Crevasse Rescue

Crevasses are deep cracks in glaciers that pose significant hazards. Proper training in crevasse rescue techniques is essential for glacier travel.

  • Always rope up when traveling on glaciers
  • Maintain proper spacing between team members
  • Carry appropriate rescue equipment
  • Practice crevasse rescue techniques regularly
  • Learn to build snow anchors and pulley systems
